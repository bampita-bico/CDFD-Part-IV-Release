\begin{abstract}
This paper asks whether Constraint-Driven Flux Dynamics (CDFD) and Adaptive Flux Limitation (AFL) can give the named system a sharper audit language. The working variables are driving flux $\Phi$, constraint $C$, surface responsiveness $S$, and structural memory $M_s$. The useful question is plain: what can be measured, what would count as overload, and what result would make the mapping fail?
\end{abstract}

\subsection{The Mujjabi Universal Capacity Law}
Building directly upon the Fundamental Physics (Part I) \cite{MujjabiPartI2026}, the \textbf{Mujjabi Universal Capacity Law} proposes that across all scales---from black hole event horizons to global financial markets and power grids---driving high flux ($\Phi$) against a rigid topological constraint ($C$) can drive system saturation. When $\Phi/C$ approaches critical thresholds, the system does not scale linearly; it undergoes a topological phase transition.

\subsection{The Mujjabi Universal Memory Law}
The \textbf{Mujjabi Universal Memory Law} frames persistent systemic collapse. When a complex network fails to clear its structural memory ($M_s$) and its relaxation parameter decays ($\tau_{relax} \to \infty$), temporary stressors become permanent rigidities. This is the proposed CDFD mechanism behind ecological death, economic depressions, and infrastructural gridlock.

A city is a thermodynamic vortex. It exists to compress humans into a small spatial area, vastly reducing the physical distance between nodes to hyper-accelerate the exchange of ideas and capital. This compression is what generates the superlinear scaling of economic output.

However, this immense compression generates massive internal pressure. Under CDFD, a city is a delicate biological membrane attempting to contain and process a profoundly energetic fluid dynamics problem.

\section{The Metropolitan Adaptive Surface}
We apply the Universal Adaptive Ratio to urban economics:
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_s = \left( \frac{\Phi}{C} \right) \cdot S \cdot M_s
\end{equation}

For a major metropolitan area:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flux ($\Phi$)}: The intensity of social interaction, business creation, and capital transfer. The "energy" of the city.
    \item \textbf{Constraint ($C$)}: The scarcity of physical land, housing units, and zoning restrictions limiting vertical growth.
    \item \textbf{Responsiveness ($S$)}: The elasticity of the housing market and public transit, enabling the city to absorb new residents without friction.
    \item \textbf{Memory ($M_s$)}: Historic preservation laws, NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) lobbying, and rigid neighborhood demographics. High memory locks the city's topology into its historical shape, refusing to adapt to modern flux.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Housing Crisis as Topological Necrosis}
When a city experiences a massive economic boom (e.g., a tech hub like San Francisco), the capital and population flux $\Phi$ skyrockets. In a healthy adaptive system, the responsiveness $S$ would flex: the city would rapidly construct high-density housing to absorb the pressure.

However, in many modern cities, the memory term $M_s$ is absolute. Historic zoning laws rigidly lock the constraints $C$, making new construction illegal or impossibly slow. Because the physical structure cannot flex, the intense pressure $\Phi$ violently impacts the fixed constraint. The adaptive ratio hits an extreme bottleneck.

The system adapts via a pathological price mechanism. Housing costs skyrocket to artificially kill the incoming flux $\Phi$ and restore equilibrium. The working-class population is purged from the membrane (gentrification), and the city loses its foundational socioeconomic diversity. The core of the city enters a state of high-cost stagnation, choking on its own topological memory.



\section{Measurement Layer}

The first job in any domain is to choose proxies before drawing conclusions. $\Phi$ may be a rate of material, energy, information, capital, or signal flow. $C$ is the bottleneck that slows or redirects that flow. $S$ measures the system's ability to reconfigure under stress, and $M_s$ records the past load that still changes present behavior. A useful application gives those four terms observable definitions, a time scale, and a failure condition. If a standard domain model explains the same data more cleanly, the CDFD/AFL mapping should give way.

\section{Conclusion}
Urban economic crises are not failures of capitalism; they are failures of topology. A city cannot simultaneously maximize socioeconomic flux and lock its spatial constraints in concrete without triggering catastrophic structural rupture. CDFD models the condition that that for a city to survive its own growth, it must actively erase its historical zoning memory ($M_s$) and maintain high topological elasticity ($S$).
