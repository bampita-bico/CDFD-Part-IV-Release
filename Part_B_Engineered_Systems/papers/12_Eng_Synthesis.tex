\begin{abstract}
This paper closes the Part B engineered-systems sequence. It treats
infrastructure, electrical grids, routing networks, distributed computation,
cloud systems, knowledge graphs, autonomous systems, and computational
architecture as engineering examples of the same CDFD/AFL modelling form. The
claim is not that these systems share one material mechanism. The narrower
claim is that throughput $\Phi$, constraint $C$, responsiveness $S$, and
structural memory $M_s$ provide a common audit language for overload, backlog,
fragility, and recovery. Release-local runtime outputs are model diagnostics and
tests that could break the mapping, not engineering certification.
\end{abstract}

\paragraph{Greek-symbol convention.}
Unless a manuscript states a tighter equation-local meaning, Greek symbols are
local CDFD variables or coefficients, not universal constants. Here $\Phi$
denotes driving flux, $\Psi_s$ denotes the adaptive operating ratio, and
$\Lambda$ denotes the Life Number where used \cite{MujjabiPartII2026}. The coefficients $\alpha$,
$\beta$, and $\gamma$ denote accumulation or reinforcement, relaxation or decay,
and diffusion, coupling, or redistribution. Subscripts restrict coefficients to
the named pathway, device, network, or experiment.

\section{Purpose}

Part B translates Adaptive Flux Limitation (AFL) into engineered systems. The
sequence begins with physical infrastructure and electrical grids, moves
through routing, distributed computation, cloud systems, knowledge graphs, and
autonomous systems, then ends with hardware architecture. This synthesis keeps
the public release boundary clear: CDFD/AFL supplies a modelling vocabulary, not
operational design authority.

\section{Common Engineering Map}

\begin{table}[h]

\midrule
$\Phi$ & throughput or demand drive & current, packets, jobs, vehicles, queries \\

$S$ & responsiveness & rerouting, autoscaling, buffering, adaptive control \\

The shared model is useful only after each term is tied to measurable proxies
and failure conditions. Without those proxies, the mapping remains an analogy.

\section{Sequence-Level Lessons}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Infrastructure and grids show the cost of fixed physical constraints
    when flow surges faster than capacity can relax.
    \item Internet, cloud, and distributed-compute papers show how software
    systems can trade statefulness for responsiveness.
    \item Knowledge graphs and autonomous systems show that informational
    systems also carry memory, backlog, and routing constraints.
    \item Computational architecture shows the same audit pattern at chip and
    memory-hierarchy scale, where throughput is bounded by locality, power, and
    translation constraints.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Measurement Layer}

The first job in any domain is to choose proxies before drawing conclusions. $\Phi$ may be a rate of material, energy, information, capital, or signal flow. $C$ is the bottleneck that slows or redirects that flow. $S$ measures the system's ability to reconfigure under stress, and $M_s$ records the past load that still changes present behavior. A useful application gives those four terms observable definitions, a time scale, and a failure condition. If a standard domain model explains the same data more cleanly, the CDFD/AFL mapping should give way.

\section{Falsification Targets}

The Part B synthesis weakens if an engineered system cannot define measurable
throughput and constraint proxies, if those proxies fail to predict overload or
recovery behavior better than existing domain models, or if adding CDFD/AFL
variables only renames known quantities without improving auditability.

\section{Conclusion}

The engineered-systems sequence frames infrastructure, computation, networks,
and autonomy as systems where flow, constraint, responsiveness, and memory can
be audited together. That framing is useful when it produces measurable failure
conditions and reproducible diagnostics. It remains hypothesis-level until it
is compared against domain-specific data and engineering baselines.
